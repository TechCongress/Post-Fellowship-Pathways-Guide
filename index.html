Exploring Your Options: Detailed Career Paths

Below are five common career paths for fellows transitioning from Congress, each with detailed insights from alumni who have taken these routes.

Option 1: Stay on the Hill, Change Offices

Exploring other management styles and policy priorities

The thing about an institution composed of "541 small businesses" is that culture and workplace environments can vary widely among different Hill offices. If you're looking to stay, you can use the Hill's high turnover rate to your advantage and consider applying to a new team.

Resources for Finding Hill Jobs:

  • • House Vacancy Announcement: Weekly list of open positions
  • • Senate Placement Office: Updated in real time
  • • Democrats' Resume Bank: For House and Senate positions
  • • GOP Resume Bank: For Republican Member positions
  • • House Office of Diversity and Inclusion: Resources for diverse candidates

If you are passionate about Congress and making it work better, there are avenues to pursue that passion without working on the Hill directly. Organizations involved in the Fix Congress Cohort, the Capitol Strong group, or the Tech, Science, and Data Cohort all build resources and advocate for Congressional staff from outside Congress.

Option 2: Other Government

Staying in public service, but taking a break from Congress

There's a huge government out there beyond the First Branch, and plenty of great options to work in various branches and levels.

Resources:

  • • GoGovernment.org: Overview of federal government career paths and navigating USAJobs.gov
  • • PresidentialTransition.org: Guide to exploring career paths in the Executive Branch
  • • Over 4,000 political appointments available, with room for good ideas and willingness to find common ground

Option 3: Nonprofits and Think Tanks

Focusing more deeply on a particular cause/policy area

If you're a policy specialist, or interested in advocacy in a particular area, your Hill skills might translate well to think tanks or nonprofits, giving you more bandwidth to focus on the issues. This can translate to a more academic-focused think tank setting, or a more advocacy-focused nonprofit role.

Job Search Resources:

  • • Tom Manatos: Up-to-date lists and video resources for DC-area job hunting
  • • Idealist: Good for nonprofit sector and policy opportunities (leans slightly left)
  • • Conservative Jobs: Posts in the more right-leaning policy world

Option 4: Back to School

Building up particular skills to switch or advance your career

Grad school is worth the time and expense if you have a strong sense of why you are going.

Questions to Ask Yourself:

  • • Is a business degree going to let you start your own company, or work in a market you love?
  • • Is a law degree going to open up new doors for legal practice and legislating?
  • • Is an MPA or MPP going to be a requirement for the kind of government work you see yourself doing?
  • • Do the think tank jobs you gravitate towards require an advanced degree?

Keep in mind: Going to grad school doesn't necessarily have to mean taking time out of your career. Plenty of great programs offer online and/or night and weekend classes, meaning that you can pursue a grad degree while keeping your career moving.

Option 5: Start Your Own Business

Going after the idea you can't get out of your head

You've written the white papers for your boss on how expanding American entrepreneurship is key to the Future of Work, and you may even have saved your precious few off-hours to pursue a side hustle. What about starting your own business?

It's becoming more common for staffers with a good idea to give it a go on their own — sometimes even working to solve problems they experienced on the Hill. TourTrackr founder Melissa Dargan left to create a tool to help offices manage their tour requests and booking, inspired by an elevator ride with Bill Gates.

Resources to Get Started:

  • • Local Chamber of Commerce: Can connect you with entrepreneurship mentoring programs
  • • Local SBA Office: Offers counseling and resources for new businesses
  • • Your Hill contacts and approach can help you get started — leverage your network!

Remember: These Are Guides, Not Rules

Career paths are rarely linear, and many fellows combine elements from multiple personas. Use these profiles as inspiration and starting points, but don't feel constrained by them. Your unique combination of interests, skills, and experiences will shape your own distinctive path.

Informed by insights from TechCongress alumni:

  • • Do your research: Use the salary ranges below as starting points, but also research on Glassdoor, Levels.fyi (for tech), and network with alumni

  • • Be confident but gracious: You deserve fair compensation for your skills, but maintain positive relationships
  • • Get it in writing: Ensure all negotiated terms are included in your offer letter

Negotiation Tips for Tech Companies

Tech companies often have room for negotiation. Don't forget to consider equity, bonuses, and benefits. Location matters significantly.

Know Your Market Value
  • • Research average salaries and total compensation (base, bonus, equity) for similar policy roles at tech companies, considering location, company size, and specific experience (e.g., AI, privacy law).
  • • Use specific tools like Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, and LinkedIn Salary Insights.
  • • Check if the company operates in a state with pay transparency laws, which may require them to provide a salary range in the job posting.
Determine Your "Thrill Number"
  • • Establish a realistic but high target salary based on your research and financial needs. Aim for a figure slightly higher than the market rate to give room for negotiation (e.g., 20% more than the initial offer).
Avoid Stating Your Price First
  • • When asked about your salary expectations, deflect the question. Instead, ask the recruiter for the role's salary range or the company's determined offer. This prevents you from lowballing yourself. A good response is: "I'm more interested in the role and the fit right now. Could you share the compensation range budgeted for this position?"
Line Up Multiple Offers
  • • The best leverage in any negotiation is a competing offer. Apply to several jobs simultaneously to coordinate offers within a similar timeframe, creating a sense of urgency for your preferred company.
Negotiation Tactics
  • • Always Negotiate: Most hiring managers have wiggle room in their initial offer and expect a counteroffer. Do not accept the first offer immediately.
  • • Focus on Total Compensation: In tech, the base salary is only one part of the package. Negotiate the entire compensation structure, including: Base Salary, Equity/Stock Options (RSUs), Signing Bonuses, Performance Bonuses, Benefits (Health, 401k match, PTO), and Perks (Remote work stipends, professional development funds).
  • • Leverage Your Unique Value and Policy Expertise: Policy roles often require specialized regulatory knowledge and strategic thinking. Highlight your specific accomplishments and how your expertise directly benefits the company's business goals and mitigates regulatory risk, using concrete data and examples to support your request.
  • • Be Professional and Confident: Maintain a polite, respectful, and collaborative tone throughout the process. Frame the negotiation as a discussion to find a mutually beneficial agreement, not an adversarial debate.
  • • Ask for the Offer in Writing: Once you receive a verbal offer, ask for all the details in a formal written document (email is fine). This ensures clarity and gives you time to review the entire package thoroughly before committing.
  • • Take Your Time: Ask for a few days to review the offer. Use this time to think clearly and strategize your counteroffer.
  • • Negotiate Over the Phone, Confirm via Email: Phone calls build rapport, but email provides a written record of the agreed-upon terms. After a phone discussion with new terms, send a follow-up email summarizing the conversation and confirming the new details.

Negotiation Tips for Congress

Government salaries are often more rigid with less room for negotiation. However, benefits and job security can be excellent. Consider the long-term trajectory.

What key factors matter most when negotiating salary on the Hill?
  • • Your portfolio and its complexity: The more "hot" or politically sensitive your issue areas are (e.g. Dept of VA tech oversight, A.I., cyber, healthcare IT, appropriations, etc.), the more leverage you have. Offices will pay to avoid risk, embarrassment, or gaps in expertise.
  • • The Member's seniority and budget: Leadership offices, committee offices, and high-profile Senators/Representatives generally have more flexibility. Freshman offices usually have the tightest caps.
  • • Timing: The best moments to negotiate are at hiring, during budget resets at the start of a Congress, or immediately after you deliver a major win (e.g., a hearing saved them, a bill you wrote is getting traction, or you fixed a political problem).
  • • Your replaceability: If they know it will take months to find someone with your subject-matter knowledge, that's leverage. Staff with technical portfolios—appropriations, cyber, EHR modernization, AI, healthcare—tend to be much harder to replace.
  • • In the House, use the Member Office Staff Salary Analysis that the CAO puts out to ensure you're asking for something within the ballpark of what is normal. You can also use the quarterly expenditure reports to find an office with a similar profile (i.e., members similarly situated in tenure, committee workload, etc.). Ask for something comparable to your peers in job title and experience and be honest about where you are in your career. While sometimes you can get lucky with an office willing to spend more than average, asking for something unreasonable comes across as inexperienced or out of touch.
Tips to effectively communicate your value in salary discussions
  • • Quantify your impact: Not just "I manage the tech portfolio," but: "I delivered the Chairman's hearing strategy, drafted three bills, managed stakeholder engagement with VA, DoD, and GAO, and prevented X issue from becoming a press headache."
  • • Connect your value to the Member's goals: Leadership, committee visibility, district priorities, media wins, avoiding pitfalls—all of that matters more to bosses than abstract productivity.
  • • Use external validation: If GAO cites your work, stakeholders praise your oversight, or other offices try to recruit you, that's legitimate leverage.
  • • Frame your request in terms of retention, not entitlement: "I love this work and want to stay, but the current salary doesn't align with the level of responsibility I'm carrying."
  • • If the salary is not communicated up front in a job description (it should be, but usually isn't), then it is ok to contact the office to ask for a salary range before applying for a job. Many will give you that information. If they do not, politely ask at the end (end!) of the initial interview or screening call when you've been given a chance to ask questions. Do not negotiate at this point; if the range is not something you can make work, can kindly let the interviewer know and thank them for their time anyway. To actually negotiate a salary, wait until you've been made an offer.
How do you evaluate and decide on a salary offer in Hill positions?
  • • Portfolio quality: Is it something that will make you marketable—national security, cyber, AI, appropriations, health, tech modernization—or something that boxes you in?
  • • Visibility and proximity to the Member: Committee and Member-adjacent roles create better career trajectories (and resume lines) than backbench portfolio work.
  • • Career runway: Will this job position you for a future LD role, committee slot, agency detail, or private-sector jump?
  • • Workload-to-salary ratio: Some offices pay average salaries but demand LD-level output. Others pay modestly but actually protect your time. Decide what matters more to you.
  • • The office culture: A supportive chief of staff and LD are worth more than an extra $5–10k. Toxic offices burn people out and stall careers.
  • • Do not respond right away to an offer when it is made, but ask to think about it (unless it is basically what you already want). When you make a counter, be sure to explain how much you want the job and then provide a basis for the amount you're asking for. The basis can be for personal situation reasons, but it must be within the ballpark of what is comparable for someone in this role. To decide whether a salary is fair, also look to people in comparable roles with publicly available information (the CAO's Member Office Staff Salary Analysis, expenditure reports, or even Legistorm).
  • • Beyond salary, when deciding whether to take a job, you should also evaluate your experience with the office. If it was challenging to communicate with the office or if there were odd interactions, trust your instincts and look elsewhere. If it feels like there's something strange going on, don't discount that feeling. How an office treats candidates is a good indication of how it treats employees.
Negotiating additional benefits beyond salary
  • • Most successful: Title adjustments (LA → Sr. LA; Advisor → Sr. Advisor). Sometimes titles unlock future salary growth even if the number doesn't move immediately. Professional development funding (courses, certifications, AI/cyber training, tech bootcamps)—offices often say yes to this because it improves your output. Flexible telework or compressed schedules—chiefs use this more often than people think to retain staff. Portfolio shifts—taking on a marquee issue (tax, health, cyber, modernization, AI) boosts your internal value and future salary.
  • • Occasionally successful: Retention bumps mid-year (rare but possible in well-funded offices). Performance-based raises tied to specific deliverables.
  • • Almost never successful: Additional PTO, bonuses, or anything that resembles private-sector benefits.
  • • You should make any unusual or unexpected ask after being made an offer. It is extremely off-putting to make demands or set conditions of your employment prior to being made an offer. The office has to want you first before it will care about what you want. (Maybe the world shouldn't work this way, but it does.) If you get the office excited about wanting you, then it is more likely to accommodate your needs, salary or otherwise.

Negotiation Tips for Advocacy & Nonprofits

Nonprofit salaries vary widely by organization size and funding. Emphasize your unique skills and the value you bring. Some flexibility in title vs. compensation.

Do your research
  • • Doing your research can give you a good starting point, as well as the extra boost of confidence that comes from truly knowing your stuff! Salary tools like Idealist's Nonprofit Salary Explorer can be a valuable resource for your research process.
  • • You can also review what wording and tactics are most appropriate and effective for different points in the process.
Don't be afraid to talk about money
  • • The truth is, the "right" time to bring up salary may vary depending on the job and the interview process. It has become more common for salary to come up as early as the initial pre-screen interview, and more and more states are requiring employers to list a salary range in the job listing.
  • • If your interviewer brings up salary expectations, answer honestly. If they raise the topic and you feel comfortable starting that conversation (and are prepared to do so), go for it! A good time may be at the very end of your interview when your interviewer shares the next steps in the hiring process. You can ask a question, like "Would you be able to give me an idea of the salary range for this position, just to make sure we're on the same page?"
  • • If you think you should wait to discuss salary, follow your instincts. If you can afford to wait it out, or you already have an idea of the salary, you may choose to kick the can down the road a bit and wait until you have an offer. Even though it shouldn't be, bringing up salary too early in an interview process (especially considering your timing and delivery) can be off-putting for an interviewer.
  • • If you do discuss compensation, we recommend speaking in terms of a range rather than a specific dollar amount. This will give you some flexibility as the interview process progresses. Consider putting the number you want at the lower end of the range, and then going up 5-10% from there. For example, rather than asking for a static $50,000, consider saying that you're looking in the $50,000-$55,000 range, and that you may be flexible when benefits are taken into consideration (if that's true for you).
  • • Pro Tip: Now that more states are requiring employers to include salary ranges in job listings, there's a good chance that you'll already know where to set initial salary expectations before submitting an application. That's progress!
Discuss your salary range with tact and confidence
  • • You've done your research, so take comfort in the knowledge that you know what you're talking about. Be diplomatic, but don't behave as if you're doing something wrong; speak calmly and authoritatively. In fact, you can even say something like "I wanted to check in about compensation for this role to see if it lines up with my expectations and the compensation research I've already done." Just be ready to give them an answer if they happen to ask what your research uncovered.
  • • Also keep in mind that your salary potential does not need to be anchored in your salary history. Being underpaid by former employers shouldn't sentence you to a cycle of low salaries moving forward.
Negotiate professionally—but clearly
  • • Once a hiring manager has made an offer, they won't be surprised to hear you'd like to negotiate. It doesn't mean they can or will play along, but it's your right to bring up salary and advocate for yourself. Try to strike a balance of showing an understanding of where they're coming from and reiterating your own talking points.
  • • Something like, "I understand what you're saying, and I'm willing to discuss it. I'd like to talk about how we can move toward $65,000. It's the market value for the role, and it's where I should be based on my experience and achievements," shows that you're listening but you also know your worth.
  • • If you try to negotiate and an employer makes it clear that they won't budge on money, you still have options. Consider negotiating other benefits like flex time, title changes, or projects that interest you. Ask how potential future raises or bonuses might work, or about opportunities for growth, and try to get anything they're able to offer you in writing. If they can't give you answers that are sustainable for your life and wellbeing, it may be a good moment to ask yourself if this is the right fit.
  • • Compensation is important. Know your worth, and ask for it.

Negotiation Tips for Consulting

Consulting firms often have structured compensation. Your fellowship experience can help you enter at a higher level. Bonuses can be substantial.

Preparation and Research
  • • Know Your Market Value: Research typical salary ranges for policy consultants in your specific location and experience level using reliable sources like Glassdoor, Payscale, and LinkedIn Salary. Networking with peers in similar roles can provide nuanced, real-world insights that online data might miss.
  • • Benchmark for Policy Expertise: Policy consulting is specialized. Be prepared to emphasize the specific demand for your subject-matter expertise (e.g., healthcare policy, environmental regulations) and how that directly translates to client value and project success.
  • • Understand the Firm's Structure: Larger, more established consulting firms often have rigid salary bands. Recognize where you might fit within their structure. If the base salary has limitations, you can focus negotiation efforts on other components of the package.
  • • Determine Your Range: Establish a target salary range based on your research and your minimum acceptable "walk-away" number. This will give you confidence and flexibility during discussions.
During the Negotiation
  • • Wait for a Formal Offer: Avoid discussing specific numbers in early interviews if possible. Wait until the company has extended an offer; this is when you have the most leverage.
  • • Focus on Value, Not Personal Needs: Justify your desired salary by highlighting your specific achievements and the unique value you bring (e.g., "My experience in navigating complex energy regulations allows me to deliver immediate value to clients in the utilities sector"). Avoid using personal financial needs as a reason for a higher salary.
  • • Maintain a Collaborative Tone: Approach the negotiation as a professional, collaborative conversation to reach a mutually beneficial agreement. Express enthusiasm for the role and the company's mission.
  • • Be Confident with Silence: After stating your desired range or counteroffer, be comfortable with a moment of silence while the hiring manager considers it. Resist the urge to fill the silence by talking yourself down.
  • • Negotiate the Full Package: If the base salary is firm, be prepared to negotiate other elements that can add significant value to your total compensation.
Negotiating Beyond Base Salary
  • • Bonuses and Performance Incentives: In consulting, bonuses are often a significant part of compensation and can be a key area for negotiation.
  • • Professional Development: Requesting a budget for specific training, certifications, or an advanced degree can be a win-win, enhancing your skills while benefiting the firm's capabilities in the policy arena.
  • • Flexible Work Arrangements: Policy roles may offer flexibility regarding remote work or hybrid schedules. Clarifying these options adds value to your overall quality of life.
  • • Additional Time Off: Extra paid time off can be a valuable non-monetary perk to negotiate.

Negotiation Tips for Think Tanks & Research

Think tank salaries are often mission-driven. Consider the intellectual freedom, publication opportunities, and network benefits alongside compensation.

Pre-Negotiation Prep
  • • Research Market Value: Use sites like Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, or academic networks (AcademicKeys, HigherEdJobs) for similar think tank/research roles (e.g., "Research Fellow," "Policy Analyst") in your location and specialty.
  • • Document Your Value: List key achievements: publications, grants secured, policy briefs influential, data analysis skills, specialized software expertise, conference presentations. Quantify impact where possible (e.g., "helped secure $X grant," "brief used by Y policymakers").
  • • Define Total Comp: Determine your "walk-away" number for base salary, but also list priorities for benefits (health, retirement), bonuses, research budget, title, vacation, and remote work flexibility.
  • • Know the Organization: Understand if they have strict salary bands (common in some government/non-profit roles) or more flexibility.
During the Negotiation
  • • Express Enthusiasm: Start positive, reiterating excitement for the role and mission.
  • • Let Them Anchor (First): Avoid giving your number first if possible; use open-ended questions like "What is the salary range for this position?" or "Can you tell me more about the compensation structure?"
  • • Anchor High (When You Counter): If you must give a number, ask for a bit more than your target (e.g., 10-20% above initial offer) using your data to justify it.
  • • Focus on Value, Not Need: Frame your ask around the unique skills and results you bring to their goals, using phrases like, "Given my experience in [Skill X] which directly supports your [Project Y], I was hoping for a base closer to..."
  • • Negotiate the Whole Package: If salary is firm, pivot to other things: a signing bonus, professional development funds, early performance review (e.g., 6 months), better title, or research resources.
  • • Stay Professional & Collaborative: Use "we" language ("What can we do to get closer to X?"). Be polite, patient, and create a win-win.
Key Think Tank Nuances
  • • Publications/Data: Emphasize your research output and analytical skills as core assets.
  • • Policy Impact: Highlight any past work that influenced policy or public discourse.

Interview Preparation Framework

Your TechCongress fellowship has given you valuable experiences to draw from. The key is articulating them effectively:

Tips by Interview Type

General Best Practices

  • • Research the organization thoroughly - understand their recent work, challenges, and priorities
  • • Prepare specific examples from your fellowship using the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result)
  • • Be ready to explain tech concepts to non-technical audiences and vice versa
  • • Prepare thoughtful questions that show your strategic thinking
  • • Send thank you notes within 24 hours, referencing specific conversation points

For Tech Company Interviews

  • • Understand the company's policy positions and recent regulatory challenges
  • • Be prepared to discuss how you'd balance business interests with responsible policy
  • • Demonstrate your understanding of the company's products and how they relate to policy
  • • Show you can work cross-functionally with legal, communications, and product teams

For Government/Hill Interviews

  • • Know the member's/agency's priorities and recent legislative/policy work
  • • Emphasize your ability to work in fast-paced, high-pressure environments
  • • Demonstrate strong writing skills - offer to provide writing samples
  • • Show you understand the political dynamics and can navigate them appropriately

For Advocacy Organization Interviews

  • • Express genuine passion for the organization's mission
  • • Demonstrate understanding of coalition building and grassroots engagement
  • • Show how your congressional experience can advance their legislative priorities
  • • Be prepared to discuss your policy positions on relevant issues

For Consulting Interviews

  • • Expect case interviews - practice frameworks for problem-solving
  • • Emphasize your project management and client-facing skills
  • • Demonstrate analytical thinking and ability to synthesize information quickly
  • • Show you can work effectively in teams and manage multiple projects

Practice Makes Perfect

Consider doing mock interviews with fellow alumni, career coaches, or the TechCongress team. Practice articulating your fellowship experience and how it relates to the role you're applying for.

Resume Focus

How to market your TechCongress fellowship experience for Congress:

  • • Position your TechCongress fellowship prominently with a clear title: "TechCongress Fellow" or "Congressional Innovation Fellow" with the Member office and dates
  • • Lead with impact: Quantify your work (e.g., "Drafted 5 pieces of legislation," "Managed oversight of $X budget," "Advised on tech policy affecting X million constituents")

Cover Letter Tips

What to include in your cover letter for Congress:

  • • Open with why you want to serve in Congress specifically—reference the Member's priorities, district, or committee work
  • • Connect your TechCongress experience directly to the job: "During my fellowship, I managed the tech oversight portfolio, which gave me hands-on experience in [relevant skill]"
  • • Demonstrate understanding of the office's needs and challenges
  • • Show you can hit the ground running: Reference specific bills, hearings, or policy areas you've already worked on
  • • Express commitment to public service and the unique mission of the office
  • • Keep it concise (under one page) and focused on value you bring, not just what you want to learn
